Protecting Trees During Construction Projects

Published: January 10, 2025 | By Bmore Rooted

Construction projects and tree preservation might seem incompatible, but with proper planning and the right techniques, you can successfully complete your building project while protecting the valuable trees on your property. Mature trees add significant value to properties, provide environmental benefits, and often take decades to replace, making their preservation during construction a worthwhile investment.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll explore the best practices for protecting trees during construction, with a focus on how air excavation technology can make the difference between tree survival and decline.

Understanding the Risks: How Construction Damages Trees

Before we dive into protection strategies, it's essential to understand how construction activities can harm trees. The damage often isn't immediately visible, which is why many property owners don't realize their trees are in trouble until months or even years after construction is complete.

Soil Compaction

Heavy machinery, foot traffic, and material storage can compact soil, reducing the oxygen available to tree roots. Roots need oxygen to function properly, and compacted soil can suffocate them. This is one of the most common and insidious forms of construction damage because it's invisible and often goes unnoticed until the tree begins to decline.

Root Severance

Excavation for foundations, utilities, and landscaping can sever major roots, compromising the tree's stability and reducing its ability to absorb water and nutrients. A tree can lose up to 25% of its root system without visible symptoms, but beyond that threshold, decline becomes likely.

Trunk and Bark Damage

Physical impacts from equipment can wound the bark and cambium layer, creating entry points for pests and diseases. Even seemingly minor bark damage can have long-term consequences for tree health.

Grade Changes

Adding or removing soil around a tree's base can bury the root collar or expose sensitive roots, both of which can be fatal. Trees evolved to grow at a specific grade, and changing that grade can disrupt their physiology.

Establishing Tree Protection Zones (TPZ)

The first step in protecting trees during construction is establishing a Tree Protection Zone (TPZ) around each tree you want to preserve. The TPZ is an area where no construction activities, material storage, or equipment operation should occur.

The general rule of thumb is that the TPZ should extend to at least the tree's dripline (the outer edge of the canopy). However, for optimal protection, many arborists recommend extending the TPZ to 1.5 times the dripline radius or calculating it based on trunk diameter (typically 1 foot of radius per inch of trunk diameter).

Physical Barriers

Install highly visible fencing around the TPZ before construction begins. This fencing should be sturdy enough to resist accidental breaches and should remain in place throughout the entire construction period. Clear signage indicating tree protection zones helps ensure all workers understand and respect these boundaries.

The Role of Air Excavation in Tree Protection

Air excavation technology has revolutionized construction tree protection. Unlike traditional mechanical excavation methods that can sever roots and damage underground structures, pneumatic air excavation uses compressed air to gently remove soil without harming tree roots.

Utility Line Installation

When utilities need to pass through or near tree protection zones, air excavation allows for precise trenching that preserves root integrity. The air excavation process exposes roots without cutting them, allowing contractors to route lines around the root system rather than through it.

Foundation Work Near Trees

For foundation excavation near protected trees, air excavation can safely expose and preserve major roots. Structural roots can be temporarily supported or protected during foundation work, then carefully reburied with proper soil conditions once construction is complete.

Soil Decompaction

After construction is complete, air excavation can be used to decompact soil within the TPZ, restoring oxygen levels and improving soil structure without damaging the root system. This remediation work can be critical to the long-term survival of trees that endured construction stress.

Pre-Construction Tree Assessment

Before breaking ground, it's essential to have a qualified arborist assess your trees. This assessment should identify:

  • Which trees are worth preserving based on health, species, and location
  • The structural condition of each tree and any pre-existing health issues
  • Appropriate TPZ dimensions for each tree
  • Specific protection measures required for each tree
  • Potential conflicts between construction plans and tree preservation

This assessment allows you to modify construction plans if necessary and establishes a baseline for tree health that can be referenced if disputes arise about construction-related damage.

Root Pruning: When and How

Sometimes, root pruning is unavoidable during construction. When this is the case, proper technique is essential. Air excavation allows arborists to expose roots without damage, making it possible to selectively prune roots with clean cuts rather than tearing them with mechanical equipment.

Root pruning should be done several months before major construction begins, giving the tree time to develop new feeder roots before the stress of construction. Strategic root pruning can actually help trees develop a more stable root system that can better withstand construction impacts.

Protecting the Root Collar and Trunk Base

The root collar—where the trunk meets the root system—is one of the most critical areas to protect during construction. This area should never be buried under fill soil or construction debris.

If grade changes are necessary near a tree, consider using tree wells to maintain the original grade around the root collar. These wells should be large enough to extend beyond the trunk flare and should include proper drainage to prevent water accumulation.

Irrigation and Stress Management

Construction is stressful for trees, even when protection measures are in place. Proper irrigation during and after construction can help trees cope with this stress. However, be cautious not to overwater, as this can lead to root rot, especially in compacted soils.

Consider installing temporary irrigation systems within the TPZ to ensure consistent moisture levels throughout the construction period. This is especially important during hot, dry weather when construction stress is compounded by drought stress.

Post-Construction Care

Once construction is complete, don't assume your work is done. Trees that survived construction may still need several years of careful management to fully recover.

Soil Remediation

Use air excavation to decompact soil within the TPZ. This can help restore oxygen levels and improve water infiltration. In some cases, soil amendments may be necessary to restore soil health.

Mulching

Apply a 2-4 inch layer of organic mulch within the TPZ, keeping it several inches away from the trunk. This helps retain moisture, moderate soil temperature, and gradually improve soil structure as it decomposes.

Monitoring

Schedule regular inspections with a certified arborist for at least three years after construction. Early detection of decline symptoms allows for intervention before problems become irreversible.

The Bottom Line: Trees and Construction Can Coexist

With proper planning, the right technology, and experienced professionals, you don't have to choose between your construction project and your trees. Air excavation and modern tree protection techniques make it possible to preserve mature trees while completing even complex building projects.

The key is to involve arborists early in the planning process, establish and enforce strict tree protection zones, and use non-invasive excavation methods whenever working near tree roots. The investment in tree protection typically pays for itself through increased property value, reduced replacement costs, and the satisfaction of knowing you've preserved a valuable natural resource.
